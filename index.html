Functionality - primary tasks

What is the primary task or activity that your user came to do? Does your application do this?

Define a primary task or activity. All other functionality is bonus material. (Welcome to the web.)

Bonus is good! We want this additional engagement with our app but the only way to get it is to nail the primary objective.

Functionality - primary tasks

  • Who is your audience?
  • What is the purpose of your page?
  • How could your current content better achieve that?
  • What is the primary task, or most common task, a user of the page will do?
  • Are there secondary tasks? What portion of users will come for those tasks?
  • Have you received specific feedback from your users that should be considered?

Functionality - secondary tasks

Are your secondary tasks getting in the way of the primary objective?

Secondary functionality should always complement the primary task, be well thought out, function smoothly, and never impede the primary functionality.

For example, links should take the user to more detailed information and be placed in context. Don't link to someone's home page, link to detailed content that complements subject matter.

Personas

Creation of fictional characters with real world, well defined tasks and abilities.

Personas can be use to define a user's goals, skills, background, experience. A named persona will create a shared understanding of who the audience is.

"Cecil the citizen" is an MPCA classic and one of several "general public" personas we've used.

Don't fall into the "general public" trap! Personas require detail, be specific.

Search Engine Optimization

How do people find information?

  • Topics have keywords. Use them often.
  • Applications are not topics.
  • Applications live within larger contextual resources like, web sites, organizations, and messaging/ advertising campaigns.
  • Inbound and out bound links drive search engine algorithms. Where are people going to find information on your topic? Put your links there.
  • Integrate keywords into your message.
  • Use Google's predictive search to research keywords.

Writing for the web

Think of writing for the web as opening up information to a wider audience.

  • Identify your key audience. Who are they and what do they want to know?
  • Put the most important information first and include your topic's keywords.
  • Example: MPCA’s page "Metro Area Chloride Project." A simple title change to “Road salt and water quality” tells visitors more about the “what and why” of the project.

credit: MPCA Communications Team guidelines

Writing for the web

  • Use plain language: Be concise. Avoid long paragraphs and run-on sentences. Each paragraph should contain one main idea and use simple sentence structure.
  • Use active verbs and avoid passive voice.
  • Avoid jargon and spell out acronyms.
  • Write for visual scanning. Use headings, subheadings, unordered lists, and informative captions.
  • Update often, a web page is only useful if it is up-to-date and informative.

Accessibility

“Accessibility is more than alt reading of text/image -- there's a substantial portion on required dexterity, being able to click on a point.” – Glenn Meyer, MPCA Communications

Web maps are inherently difficult from an accessibility standpoint. While some things can be done to address this, it’s best to:

  • Supplement the map with a description and/or table
  • Keep the user within a single accessibility paradigm. By “accessibility paradigm” I mean, don’t make them jump back and forth between websites that may provide different solutions to accessibility issues.

Affirmations and other heuristics

Good design makes hard things easy.

Keep a bias toward simplicity.

It’s okay to say no, just because someone asks for a feature doesn’t mean you should do it. Every feature has a cost that goes beyond the initial implementation: maintenance cost, documentation cost, and cognitive cost for your users. Always ask: Should we really do this? Often, the answer is simply no.

When you say yes to a request for supporting a new use case, remember that literally adding what the user requested is often not the optimal choice. Users are focused on their own specific use case, and you must counter this with a holistic and principled vision of the whole project. Often, the right answer is to extend an existing feature.

Always seek to minimize the cognitive load imposed on your users in the course of using your API. Automate what can be automated, minimize the actions and choices needed from the user, don’t expose options that are unimportant, design simple and consistent workflows.

Deliberately design end-to-end workflows, not a set of atomic features. Most developers approach API design by asking: What capabilities should be available? Let’s have configuration options for them. Instead, ask: What are the use cases for this tool? For each use case, what is the optimal sequence of user actions? What’s the easiest API that could support this workflow?
